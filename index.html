Treaty 1

What is Treaty 1?

Treaties are agreements made between the Canadian government and the Indigenous people who were already living on the land. These agreements were usually about sharing the land and protecting their rights. Treaty 1 is one of the first treaties that was negotiated and signed in 1871. The area of Treaty 1 covered most of southern Manitoba, including cities like Winnipeg and Brandon, along with other municipalities. Today, treaty 1 is now a part of the province of Manitoba, and some of the treaty terms are still in effect, but most of it is now gone.

Where was Treaty 1 signed?

The treaty was signed at Lower Fort Garry, also known as the Stone Fort. It is located near the town of Selkirk, northeast of Winnipeg. The Canadian government chose Lower Fort Garry because it was a convenient and strategic meeting place. Lower Fort Garry today is now a historic site and a museum that has history of the signing. The place even has reenactors simulating what it was like to be there back then.

Who signed Treaty 1?

Treaty 1 was signed by the Canadian government and the Indigenous peoples. The treaty involved the Anishinaabe, Swampy Cree, and the Oji-Cree people. The bands that participated are the Peguis, Sagkeeng, Brokenhead, Roseau River, and the Portage band, with Adams Archibald as the lieutenant governor, Wemyss Simpson as the commissioner, and James McKay as the translator, and Chief Peguis who represented the Indigenous people.

Why was Treaties made?

The numbered treaties were originally made by the Canadian government as they wanted to have more space for cities, farms, and railways to be built as more settlers were traveling to North America, while the first nations were given things such as reserved land, schools, and other needs. So the numbered treaties were made so the Canadians and the Indigenous people can both live on the land. Treaties was also made to make sure that the western part would not be claimed by the United States.
